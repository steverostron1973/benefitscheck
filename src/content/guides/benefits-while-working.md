One of the biggest myths about the UK benefits system is that working means you can't claim anything. In reality, millions of working people are entitled to financial support — and a significant number never claim it.

This guide covers the benefits most commonly missed by people in employment.

## Universal Credit top-up

If you're working but on a low income, you may still be entitled to Universal Credit. The system is specifically designed to top up low wages rather than replace them entirely. Your payment reduces gradually as you earn more, rather than stopping the moment you start work.

Many part-time workers, zero-hours contract workers, and people on minimum wage are entitled to a UC top-up and don't realise it. If your household income is below a certain threshold after housing costs, it's worth checking.

## Council Tax Reduction

Council Tax Reduction is means-tested, not work-tested. Whether you're working or not, if your income is low enough relative to your council tax bill, your local council may reduce your bill — in some cases to zero.

Each council runs its own scheme with its own rules, so eligibility varies by area. But this is one of the most widely unclaimed benefits among working people on lower incomes.

## Child Benefit

Child Benefit isn't means-tested at the lower end — anyone responsible for a child under 16 (or under 20 in approved education or training) can claim it, regardless of whether they work or how much they earn.

The catch: if you or your partner earns over £60,000 a year, a High Income Child Benefit Charge gradually claws it back. But below that threshold, you should be claiming it. Many families with higher earners have stopped claiming unnecessarily — the threshold increased significantly in 2024.

## PIP (Personal Independence Payment)

PIP is one of the most misunderstood benefits in the UK. It has nothing to do with whether you work or how much you earn — it's based entirely on how a health condition or disability affects your daily life and mobility.

You can work full time, earn a good salary, and still be entitled to PIP if your condition meets the criteria. Many working people with long-term conditions — including mental health conditions, chronic pain, and neurodivergent conditions — are entitled to PIP and never claim it.

## Carer's Allowance

If you spend at least 35 hours a week caring for someone who receives certain disability benefits, you may be entitled to Carer's Allowance — even if you're also working. The only earnings limit is £196 net per week (2026/27 rate) after allowable deductions. Many part-time carers who also work don't realise they fall under this threshold.

## Free childcare hours

Working parents are entitled to government-funded childcare hours for children aged 9 months to 4 years. The amount depends on your child's age and your working hours. This isn't a cash benefit but can be worth thousands of pounds a year in reduced childcare costs — and take-up is lower than it should be, particularly for the 9-month to 2-year entitlement which is relatively new.

## How to check what you're entitled to

The best way to find out which of these applies to your situation is to check each one individually based on your household circumstances — income, housing costs, children, and any health conditions.
