Chronic pain is one of the most challenging conditions to claim PIP for — not because it does not qualify, but because it is invisible, fluctuating, and hard to measure. Many people receive lower awards than they are entitled to because they describe the pain itself rather than what they cannot do.

## What counts as chronic pain for PIP

Chronic pain conditions that commonly appear in PIP claims include fibromyalgia, CRPS, chronic back pain, neuropathic pain, endometriosis, chronic headache and migraine, and pain linked to arthritis, MS, or injury. You do not need a specific diagnosis — if pain significantly affects daily living or mobility on the majority of days, you may qualify whether or not an underlying cause has been identified.

## How chronic pain typically affects PIP activities

Below are the activities most often affected, with the descriptors and point values that typically apply.

### Preparing food

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points.** Perching stool, adapted knives, or jar openers.
- **Needs prompting to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points.** Pain distraction, medication side effects, or exhaustion mean you need reminding to cook or eat.
- **Needs supervision or assistance to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 4 points.** Someone helps because pain makes chopping, lifting, or concentrating on the hob unsafe.
- **Cannot prepare and cook a simple meal — 8 points.** On most days you cannot cook safely even with aids.

Someone with chronic back pain who can start chopping vegetables but must stop twice because of spasm, takes 40 minutes instead of 15, and then needs to lie flat for an hour is not completing the activity reliably.

### Washing and bathing

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to wash or bathe — 2 points.** Shower seat, grab rails, or long-handled sponge.
- **Needs supervision or prompting to be able to wash or bathe — 2 points.** Someone nearby because of dizziness from pain medication, or prompting because pain makes initiating washing overwhelming.
- **Needs assistance to be able to wash either their hair or body below the waist — 2 points.** Reaching up or bending down is too painful without help.
- **Needs assistance to be able to get in or out of a bath or shower — 3 points.**
- **Cannot wash and bathe at all and needs another person to wash their entire body — 8 points.**

### Dressing and undressing

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to dress or undress — 2 points.** Sock aids, dressing sticks, or button hooks because bending or fine grip triggers pain.
- **Needs either prompting or assistance to be able to select appropriate clothing — 2 points.** Pain fog or sensory sensitivity means you need help choosing clothes you can tolerate.
- **Needs assistance to be able to dress or undress their upper body — 2 points** or **lower body — 2 points.** Someone helps with bras, jumpers, trousers, or shoes because raising arms or bending is unbearable.
- **Cannot dress or undress at all — 8 points.** On most days you cannot dress yourself even with aids.

Someone with fibromyalgia or widespread pain who needs to rest twice while getting dressed and takes 40 minutes instead of 10 would likely score on the dressing descriptor.

### Managing therapy or monitoring a health condition

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to manage medication — 1 point.** Dosette boxes or alarms because pain medication regimes are complex or pain fog causes missed doses.
- **Needs supervision, prompting or assistance to manage medication — 1 point.** Someone prompts timing or checks doses.

Higher therapy descriptors (2–8 points) may apply if you need help with physiotherapy, pacing programmes, or TENS/heat routines for several hours a week.

### Engaging with other people face to face

- **Needs prompting to be able to engage with other people — 2 points.** Pain, exhaustion, or low mood mean you need encouragement to answer the door or attend appointments.
- **Needs social support to be able to engage with other people — 4 points.** Face-to-face contact only manageable with a familiar person present.
- **Cannot engage with other people due to such engagement causing either overwhelming psychological distress to the claimant or a risk to the claimant or another person — 8 points.**

### Planning and following journeys

- **Needs prompting to be able to undertake any journey to avoid overwhelming psychological distress — 4 points.** Fear of a pain flare away from home means you need encouragement to go out.
- **Cannot follow the route of an unfamiliar journey without another person, assistance dog or orientation aid — 10 points.** Pain distraction, medication effects, or panic in unfamiliar places mean you cannot navigate alone.
- **Cannot undertake any journey because it would cause overwhelming psychological distress — 10 points.** On most days leaving the house causes such distress that you cannot go out.

### Moving around

Pain on walking is one of the most direct impacts.

- **Can stand and then move more than 50 metres but no more than 200 metres, either aided or unaided — 4 points.**
- **Can stand and then move unaided more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 8 points.**
- **Can stand and then move using an aid or appliance more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 10 points.**
- **Can stand and then move more than 1 metre but no more than 20 metres, either aided or unaided — 12 points.**

Describe how far you can walk *reliably* — safely, repeatedly, without a long recovery — not the furthest you managed on a rare good day. If walking from the car to the front door is near your limit on most days, that sits in the 20–50 metre range (8 or 10 points depending on aids).

## The safety criterion — crucial for chronic pain

One of the four reliability tests is safety: can you complete an activity without unreasonable risk of harm? For chronic pain this matters often. If pushing through pain could cause injury, a fall, or a severe flare, or if pain distracts you when using sharp knives or hot pans, say so. “I can do it if I push through” is not the same as completing the activity safely.

## The majority of days rule and chronic pain

PIP is assessed on how you are on the **majority of days** over a 12-month period — not your best days and not just assessment day. Chronic pain often fluctuates. If significant limitation occurs on more than half your days across the year, that pattern should drive which descriptors apply.

### How to document fluctuation

A pain diary is one of the strongest tools for a chronic pain claim. Keep it for at least 2–4 weeks before your form, and bring a summary to your assessment. For each day, note:

- Pain level (0–10) and location
- What triggered or worsened it (standing, bending, walking, stress, weather)
- Fatigue and sleep quality
- Which activities you could and could not complete
- How long tasks took, rests needed, and recovery time
- Medication taken and side effects (drowsiness, dizziness, nausea)
- Aids used, help from another person, or activities abandoned

Avoid “it varies.” Say: “On roughly 5 out of 7 days my lower back and leg pain means I cannot walk more than 50 metres without stopping and I need a shower seat to wash. On the remaining 2 days I can manage short walks with a stick, but I still need to rest afterwards and cannot cook standing at the hob.” That maps onto descriptors.

Also describe cumulative effect: pain manageable for the first five minutes but disabling by the end still means you cannot complete the activity repeatedly or to an acceptable standard.

## What to write on your PIP form

Assessors need what you cannot do — not only that you are in pain.

**Don't write:** “I struggle with walking.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days I am unable to walk more than 20 metres without stopping due to pain and fatigue. If I push further I pay for it with a flare lasting the rest of the day. I use a stick outdoors.”

**Don't write:** “Cooking hurts.”  
**Do write:** “On most days I cannot prepare and cook a simple meal safely because standing and gripping cause severe pain. I need a perching stool and often need someone else to cook, or I eat ready meals. After any attempt I usually need to lie down.”

**Don't write:** “Showering is hard.”  
**Do write:** “On most days I need a shower seat and grab rails. Bending and raising my arms cause significant pain. A shower takes 30–40 minutes including rests, then I usually need to sleep.”

**Don't write:** “I’m in constant pain.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days pain at 7/10 or above stops me completing dressing without rests, cooking without help, and walking more than about 40 metres. After morning personal care I cannot manage other activities until I have rested for several hours.”

Use “on the majority of days” or a clear weekly pattern; cover safely, to an acceptable standard, repeatedly, and in a reasonable time; describe what happens *after* activity; name aids and help from others.

## What award might you expect?

Moderate chronic pain affecting one or two activities may score below 8 points. Severe pain affecting multiple activities daily may attract Standard or Enhanced Daily Living (8+ / 12+), with Mobility depending on walking distance and journey anxiety. Points often add up across preparing food, washing, dressing, and moving around.

## Claiming while working

PIP has no earnings or hours limit. Many people with chronic pain work at significant personal cost and may still qualify. Describe pacing, adaptations, crashing after shifts, and how that leaves daily activities unmanageable at home.

## What evidence helps your claim

Focus on functional impact: GP letter covering severity and what you cannot do; pain clinic letters; medication evidence; physio/OT records; a pain diary; evidence of aids. Ask clinicians to comment on walking limits, need for help, and bad-day frequency — not only to confirm chronic pain.

## Common mistakes

**Describing only the pain, not the impact.** For every activity, say specifically what you cannot do.  
**Not describing the cumulative effect.** Can you finish the task, repeat it, and function afterwards?  
**Underreporting on good days.** Base answers on the majority of days across the year.  
**Not mentioning fatigue.** Pain-related exhaustion often stops you repeating activities.  
**Minimising medication side effects.** Drowsiness or dizziness can affect safety and prompting needs.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Keep your pain diary and clinic letters between reviews. If pain worsens, report a change of circumstances and request an earlier reassessment.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I get PIP for chronic pain if scans are “normal”?

Yes. PIP is based on functional impact, not scan findings. Describe what you cannot do on the majority of days and support it with GP letters, medication records, and a diary.

### Will I fail if I look well or manage to walk into the assessment?

No. Appearance on the day is not the test. Explain how you got there (painkillers, resting beforehand, recovering afterwards) and stick to your majority-of-days description.

### Does “pushing through” the pain mean I won’t score points?

No. If you only complete an activity by accepting injury risk, a severe flare, or being unable to function afterwards, you are not completing it reliably. Describe that cost.

### How many points do I need, and what are the rates?

8 points for Standard and 12 for Enhanced on each component. For 2026/27: Standard Daily Living £72.65 a week, Enhanced £108.55; Standard Mobility £28.70, Enhanced £75.75.

### Does it matter which pain condition I have?

The diagnosis helps explain your pattern, but scoring is based on how pain affects each activity. Describe your specific limits activity by activity — for example walking with back pain, or dressing and gripping with widespread or neuropathic pain.
