Chronic pain is one of the most challenging conditions to claim PIP for — not because it doesn't qualify, but because it's invisible, fluctuating, and difficult to measure objectively. Many people with chronic pain receive lower awards than they're entitled to because they struggle to describe their limitations effectively. Understanding the system makes a real difference.

## What counts as chronic pain for PIP

Chronic pain conditions that commonly appear in PIP claims include fibromyalgia, complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS), chronic back pain, neuropathic pain, endometriosis, chronic headache and migraine, and pain associated with conditions like arthritis or MS.

You don't need a specific chronic pain diagnosis to claim — if pain significantly affects your ability to carry out daily activities, you may qualify regardless of whether a specific underlying cause has been identified.

## How chronic pain affects PIP activities

Chronic pain can affect almost any PIP activity depending on where the pain is located and how severe it is:

**Daily Living activities commonly affected:**
- **Preparing food** — standing, gripping, lifting, and using kitchen equipment can all be affected by pain
- **Washing and bathing** — getting in and out of the bath, reaching to wash, and standing in the shower can all be painful
- **Dressing** — bending, reaching, and sustained postures can cause significant pain
- **Moving around the home** — pain on walking and changing position can make moving around the home slow and difficult

**Mobility activities commonly affected:**
- **Moving around** — pain on walking is one of the most direct impacts on this activity
- **Planning and following journeys** — pain unpredictability and associated anxiety can affect the ability to travel independently

## The safety criterion — crucial for chronic pain

One of the four criteria assessors use is safety — can you complete an activity without risk of harm to yourself? For chronic pain, this is particularly relevant. If pushing through pain to complete an activity could cause injury, or if pain distracts you in ways that create safety risks (for example, when using sharp kitchen implements), this should be described.

## The majority of days rule and chronic pain

Chronic pain often fluctuates — some days better, some days significantly worse. The assessment should reflect your average across the whole year. On bad days, how limited are you? How frequently do bad days occur? This is the picture that matters, not how you are on a good day.

## What award might you expect?

Chronic pain awards vary enormously depending on the severity of the pain, how many activities it affects, and whether you need aids or help. Moderate chronic pain affecting one or two activities may not meet the threshold; severe chronic pain affecting multiple activities daily may attract Enhanced rate awards.

## Claiming while working

PIP is not affected by employment. Many people with chronic pain manage to work, often at significant personal cost, and are still entitled to PIP based on their functional limitations outside of work.

## What evidence helps your claim

- GP letter describing your pain history, current severity, and functional impact
- Letters from a pain clinic or specialist if you're under one
- Evidence of pain medication prescribed
- Physiotherapy or occupational therapy records
- A pain diary showing the pattern and severity of your pain over time — this can be particularly valuable for fluctuating conditions

## Common mistakes when claiming PIP for chronic pain

**Describing only the pain, not the impact** — assessors need to understand what you can't do, not just that you're in pain. For every activity, describe specifically how pain limits you.

**Not describing the cumulative effect** — chronic pain often gets worse with activity. Describe not just whether you can start a task but whether you can complete it, and how you feel afterward.

**Underreporting on good days** — people naturally feel more positive and capable on good days. Try to describe your realistic average, not just how you feel when completing the form.

**Not mentioning fatigue** — chronic pain is exhausting. Fatigue from managing pain all day significantly affects daily activities and should be described.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Chronic pain conditions can improve, worsen, or change over time. Keep records of any changes in your condition, new treatments, or changes in medication between reviews.
