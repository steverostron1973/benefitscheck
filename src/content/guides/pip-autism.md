Autism spectrum conditions are increasingly recognised in PIP assessments, but many autistic people and their families still don't claim — either because they don't realise they qualify, or because they've had a previous claim refused. Understanding how autism affects PIP scoring is essential to getting a fair outcome.

## Autism and PIP — the key principle

PIP doesn't assess your diagnosis; it assesses how your condition affects your ability to carry out specific daily activities. Autism affects people very differently — some autistic people live and work largely independently; others need significant support for daily tasks. The assessment should reflect your individual experience, not assumptions based on the diagnosis alone.

## How autism typically affects PIP activities

**Daily Living activities often affected:**

- **Preparing food** — sensory sensitivities can make handling certain textures, smells, or temperatures extremely difficult; executive function difficulties can affect the ability to plan and sequence the steps of cooking safely
- **Managing therapy or monitoring a health condition** — many autistic people have complex health needs or therapy regimes that require support to manage
- **Engaging with other people** — social communication difficulties are central to autism and can significantly affect the ability to engage with others, understand social cues, and communicate needs
- **Making budgeting decisions** — executive function difficulties can affect financial management
- **Planning and following journeys** — this is one of the most commonly affected activities for autistic people; the need for routine, difficulty with unexpected changes, and anxiety about unfamiliar environments can all affect the ability to travel independently

**Mobility activities often affected:**

- **Planning and following journeys** — scored under mobility, this covers the ability to plan routes, follow unfamiliar journeys, and cope with changes. Many autistic people score significantly here due to anxiety, need for routine, and difficulty with unexpected situations.

## Sensory processing and PIP

Sensory processing differences — hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity to sound, light, texture, smell, or touch — are a significant part of many autistic people's experience and can affect multiple PIP activities. If sensory sensitivities affect your ability to prepare food, use public transport, or engage with other people, describe these clearly in your claim.

## The majority of days rule

Autism is not a fluctuating condition in the same way as some physical conditions, but many autistic people experience periods of increased difficulty — particularly during times of stress, change, or sensory overload. Describe how you are across the whole range of your experience, not just on your best days.

## What award might you expect?

Awards for autism vary very widely. An autistic person who lives independently, works, and manages daily tasks with minimal support may not meet the threshold. An autistic person who needs significant support with daily living activities and cannot travel independently may receive Enhanced rate on both components.

The journey descriptor (planning and following journeys) is often where autistic people score most significantly for mobility.

## Claiming while working

PIP is completely unaffected by employment. Many autistic people work — often in structured, routine environments that suit their strengths — while still needing significant support at home or finding daily activities genuinely difficult. Working doesn't disqualify you.

## What evidence helps your claim

- Diagnostic assessment report (your autism diagnosis documentation)
- GP letter describing how autism affects your daily functioning
- Letters from any specialists involved in your care (psychiatrist, psychologist, speech and language therapist, occupational therapist)
- Evidence of any support you receive (support worker, therapy)
- A supporting statement from a parent, partner, or carer describing what support you need day to day — this can be particularly valuable for autistic people who may find it difficult to accurately self-report their needs in a formal setting

## Common mistakes when claiming PIP for autism

**Masking in the assessment** — many autistic people, particularly those who have developed strong masking strategies, present well in formal settings. This can work against them in assessments. Be honest about the effort masking takes and how you feel before and after social interactions.

**Not mentioning the hidden effort** — if you can technically do something but it requires enormous cognitive effort, causes significant anxiety, or leaves you exhausted, that matters for PIP.

**Assuming high-functioning means not entitled** — functioning labels are not used in PIP assessments. What matters is whether you need support with specific activities, regardless of how you're perceived overall.

**Not claiming for the journey descriptor** — this is one of the highest-scoring areas for many autistic people and is frequently missed in claims.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Autism is a lifelong condition. At reassessment, you may be asked to demonstrate again how it affects you. Keep records of any support you receive and any changes in your needs between reviews.
