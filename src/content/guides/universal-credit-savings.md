One of the most common questions about Universal Credit is whether having savings disqualifies you. The answer depends on how much you have — and the rules are more nuanced than a simple yes or no.

## The three savings thresholds

Universal Credit uses a three-tier approach to savings:

**Under £6,000** — your savings are ignored completely. They have no effect on your UC claim whatsoever.

**Between £6,000 and £16,000** — your savings don't stop you claiming, but they reduce your payment. For every £250 (or part of £250) over £6,000, £4.35 per month is deducted from your award. So £7,000 in savings would result in a £17.40/month deduction (4 × £4.35).

**£16,000 or more** — you're not eligible for Universal Credit at all. This applies to your household total, including a partner's savings.

## What counts as savings (capital)

It's not just cash in the bank. Capital includes:

- Savings accounts and ISAs
- Stocks and shares
- Property you own but don't live in (valued at market rate minus 10% selling costs and any mortgage)
- Certain lump sum payments

Your home (the one you live in), personal possessions, and most pension funds don't count as capital.

## Joint claims

If you're making a joint UC claim with a partner, your savings are combined. So if you have £5,000 and your partner has £12,000, your combined capital is £17,000 — which would make you ineligible.

## If your savings fluctuate

You're required to report changes in your savings to the DWP. If your savings drop below £16,000 — for example after paying a large bill — you may become eligible again and should make a new claim at that point.
