Self-employed people can claim Universal Credit — but the way your income is assessed is different from employees, and there's a key rule called the Minimum Income Floor that can significantly affect your award.

## How self-employment income is assessed

Unlike employees whose income is reported automatically through HMRC's Real Time Information system, self-employed UC claimants need to report their earnings monthly through their online UC journal. You report your actual income and allowable business expenses each month, and your UC payment adjusts accordingly.

Allowable expenses include things like materials, equipment, business insurance, and a proportion of phone and travel costs. You can't deduct personal expenses — only genuine business costs.

## The Minimum Income Floor

The Minimum Income Floor (MIF) is the most important rule for self-employed UC claimants to understand — and the one that catches most people out.

The MIF assumes that if you're self-employed, you earn at least the equivalent of the National Living Wage for your expected hours of work — even if your actual earnings are lower. In practice, this means:

- If you earn less than the MIF in a given month, UC treats you as if you earned the MIF amount
- Your UC payment is calculated based on this assumed income, not your actual income
- This can result in receiving less UC than you'd expect based on your real earnings

The MIF doesn't apply in your first 12 months of self-employment (the "start-up period"), or if you have a health condition that limits your ability to work.

## Monthly reporting is essential

You must report your self-employment income every month through your UC journal, even if you earned nothing. Missing a report can result in your payment being delayed or reduced. Keep clear records of all income and expenses so you can report accurately.

## What counts as self-employment

You're treated as self-employed for UC purposes if you run your own business, work as a freelancer or contractor, or work through your own limited company in some circumstances. HMRC's employment status rules apply.

## The Surplus Earnings rule

If you have an unusually high-income month — for example, a large one-off payment — the excess earnings above a certain threshold can be "banked" as surplus earnings and offset against future UC awards. This is designed to prevent people from manipulating their income across months, but it can be confusing in practice.
