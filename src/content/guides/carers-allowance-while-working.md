Carer's Allowance is one of the most misunderstood benefits in the UK. Many people assume that working automatically disqualifies them — but that's not the case. You can work and claim Carer's Allowance, as long as your net earnings stay below a certain threshold.

## The earnings limit

In 2026/27, you can earn up to £196 per week net and still claim Carer's Allowance. Net earnings means after deductions for income tax, National Insurance, and half of any pension contributions you make. Certain expenses can also be deducted if you pay for care for your child or the person you care for while you're at work.

This means someone working part-time on a low wage may well fall under the threshold — particularly after deductions.

## The 35-hour caring rule

To qualify for Carer's Allowance, you must spend at least 35 hours a week caring for someone who receives a qualifying disability benefit — usually the daily living component of PIP at either rate, or Attendance Allowance. The caring hours and working hours are separate — there's no maximum total hours rule.

## What Carer's Allowance pays

Carer's Allowance is currently £83.30 per week (2026/27). It's taxable, and it counts as income for Universal Credit purposes — though carers on UC also receive a Carer Element on top.

## The UC overlap trap

One important catch: you can't receive Carer's Allowance and certain other benefits at the same time due to the overlapping benefits rule. If you're on Universal Credit, you won't receive Carer's Allowance as a cash payment — but you'll still get the Carer Element added to your UC award, which is worth £198.31/month in 2026/27.
