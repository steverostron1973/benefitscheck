A PIP assessment is one of the most stressful parts of making a claim — but being well prepared significantly improves your chances of a fair outcome. Understanding exactly what assessors are looking for, and how to describe your condition effectively, is the single most important thing you can do.

## What the assessment is actually measuring

Assessors aren't trying to catch you out or prove you're faking. They're scoring you against 12 specific activities across two components — Daily Living and Mobility — to determine how many points you score and therefore what award level you receive.

For each activity, they're looking at whether you can complete it:
- Safely (without risk of harm)
- To an acceptable standard
- Repeatedly throughout the day
- In a reasonable time (no more than twice as long as someone without your condition)

If you can only complete an activity when all four of these are true, you may score higher than if you can technically do it but struggle with one of these factors.

## Describe your worst days, not your best

This is the most common mistake people make. PIP is assessed based on how you are on the majority of days over a 12-month period — not on the day of your assessment, and not on your best days.

If your condition fluctuates, make sure you describe how you are on bad days, not just when you're managing well. Assessors should take the full picture into account — if you push through significant pain or difficulty to complete tasks, that matters.

## Be specific and detailed

Vague answers like "I struggle to walk" are less useful than specific ones: "I can walk about 20 metres before I need to stop due to pain in my hip, and it takes me around 10 minutes to recover before I can walk again."

For every activity, think about:
- How long it takes you
- Whether you need to stop and rest
- Whether you need aids or adaptations
- Whether you need help from another person
- How you feel afterwards (fatigue, pain, anxiety)

## Don't minimise your difficulties

Many people instinctively downplay their condition — either out of habit, or because they're having a relatively good day at the assessment. Try to resist this. Be honest and accurate about your limitations.

If you've driven yourself to the assessment, that doesn't mean you can drive daily. If you walked into the building, that doesn't mean you can walk distances regularly. Assessors are trained to consider the whole picture, not just what they observe on the day.

## Bring supporting evidence

Medical evidence isn't required but it helps. Letters from your GP, consultant, or specialist — particularly ones that describe how your condition affects your daily life — can support your account. The assessment report carries significant weight, but written evidence from healthcare professionals can make a real difference at the decision stage or on appeal.

## You can bring someone with you

You're entitled to bring a friend, family member, or support worker to your assessment. Having someone with you can help you feel more confident, and they can also help if you forget something important.

## Request a copy of the assessment report

After your assessment, you can request a copy of the assessor's report. This is important if you disagree with the outcome — it helps you understand exactly how you were scored and what the assessor noted, which is essential information if you want to challenge the decision.
