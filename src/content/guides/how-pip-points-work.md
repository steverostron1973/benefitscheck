PIP — Personal Independence Payment — is not awarded based on your diagnosis. It is based on how your condition affects everyday activities, measured through a points system defined in law. This guide is a plain-English reference to how that system works, including **all 12 PIP activities** with their descriptors and point values.

## The two components of PIP

PIP has two parts, scored completely separately:

**Daily Living** — 10 activities (preparing food through to budgeting).  
**Mobility** — 2 activities (planning/following journeys, and moving around).

You can be awarded one component, both, or neither. Points from Daily Living are never added to Mobility points.

## How scoring works

For each activity, there is a list of **descriptors** — statements of what you can or cannot do. You score **only the highest descriptor that applies** within that activity (you do not add several descriptors from the same activity together). Then you **add the highest score from each activity** to get a component total.

**Daily Living thresholds:**
- 8–11 points → Standard rate (£72.65 a week, 2026/27)
- 12 or more points → Enhanced rate (£108.55 a week)

**Mobility thresholds:**
- 8–11 points → Standard rate (£28.70 a week)
- 12 or more points → Enhanced rate (£75.75 a week)

Score 7 or fewer in a component and you get nothing for that component — even if you scored 7 in both.

## The reliability test — the rule that changes awards

You are only treated as able to do an activity if you can do it:

1. **Safely** — without unreasonable risk of harm to yourself or others  
2. **To an acceptable standard**  
3. **Repeatedly** — as often as reasonably required  
4. **In a reasonable time** — no more than twice as long as someone without your condition

If you can only cook once, then crash for the rest of the day, you are not completing preparing food *repeatedly*. If dressing takes 40 minutes instead of 10, you are not doing it in a *reasonable time*. Those failures can move you onto a higher-point descriptor even if you “technically” finish the task once.

## The majority of days rule

PIP looks at how you are on the **majority of days** over a 12-month period — not your best day, not only your worst day, and not just assessment day.

If a limitation applies on more than half your days across the year, that is usually the descriptor that should apply. For fluctuating conditions, describe the realistic yearly pattern.

### How to document fluctuation

Keep a symptom or activity diary for at least 2–4 weeks. For each day, note:

- Which of the 12 activities you could and could not complete
- Aids used and help from another person
- How long tasks took and how many rests you needed
- What happened afterwards (flares, exhaustion, recovery time)
- Safety incidents (falls, burns, missed medication, getting lost)

Avoid “it varies.” Say: “On roughly 5 out of 7 days I cannot walk more than 50 metres without stopping and I need help to cook. On the other 2 days I can manage short walks with a stick but still need a shower seat.”

## Daily Living activities — full descriptors and points

### 1. Preparing food

Covers preparing and cooking a simple meal for one using fresh ingredients (not gourmet cooking).

- **Can prepare and cook a simple meal unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points**
- **Cannot cook a simple meal using a conventional cooker but is able to do so using a microwave — 2 points**
- **Needs prompting to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points**
- **Needs supervision or assistance to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 4 points**
- **Cannot prepare and cook food — 8 points**

**Example:** Someone who must stop twice while cooking, takes 45 minutes instead of 15, and then needs to lie down is not completing the activity reliably.

### 2. Taking nutrition

About eating and drinking — getting food and drink to your mouth — not cooking.

- **Can take nutrition unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to take nutrition; or supervision to take nutrition; or assistance to cut up food — 2 points**
- **Needs a therapeutic source to be able to take nutrition — 2 points**
- **Needs prompting to be able to take nutrition — 4 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to manage a therapeutic source to take nutrition — 6 points**
- **Cannot convey food and drink to their mouth and needs another person to do so — 10 points**

**Example:** Adapted cutlery or help cutting food is often 2 points; needing regular prompting to eat because of depression or cognitive problems is 4 points.

### 3. Managing therapy or monitoring a health condition

Medication, monitoring (for example blood glucose), and home therapies (physio, dressings, dialysis support).

- **Does not receive medication/therapy/monitoring, or can manage unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs an aid or appliance to manage medication; and/or supervision, prompting or assistance to manage medication; and/or supervision, prompting or assistance to monitor a health condition — 1 point**
- **Needs supervision, prompting or assistance to manage therapy that takes no more than 3.5 hours a week — 2 points**
- **… more than 3.5 but no more than 7 hours a week — 4 points**
- **… more than 7 but no more than 14 hours a week — 6 points**
- **… more than 14 hours a week — 8 points**

**Example:** Dosette boxes or prompting for tablets usually score 1 point. Regular help with lengthy home therapy can score much higher if you evidence weekly hours.

### 4. Washing and bathing

Getting in and out of an unadapted bath/shower and washing your whole body, including hair.

- **Can wash and bathe unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to wash or bathe — 2 points**
- **Needs supervision or prompting to be able to wash or bathe — 2 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to wash either their hair or body below the waist — 2 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to get in or out of a bath or shower — 3 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to wash their body between the shoulders and waist — 4 points**
- **Cannot wash and bathe at all and needs another person to wash their entire body — 8 points**

**Example:** Shower seat or grab rails = 2 points; physical help getting in/out = 3 points.

### 5. Managing toilet needs or incontinence

On/off the toilet, cleaning yourself, and managing incontinence.

- **Can manage toilet needs or incontinence unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to manage toilet needs or incontinence — 2 points**
- **Needs supervision or prompting to be able to manage toilet needs — 2 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to manage toilet needs — 4 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to manage incontinence of either bladder or bowel — 6 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to manage incontinence of both bladder and bowel — 8 points**

**Example:** Continence pads are an aid (2 points). Help managing bladder or bowel incontinence is 6 points; both is 8.

### 6. Dressing and undressing

Clothes and fastenings, including selecting appropriate clothing.

- **Can dress and undress unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to dress or undress — 2 points**
- **Needs either prompting or assistance to be able to select appropriate clothing — 2 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to dress or undress their lower body — 2 points**
- **Needs assistance to be able to dress or undress their upper body — 2 points**
- **Cannot dress or undress at all — 8 points**

**Example:** Someone who needs to rest twice while dressing and takes 40 minutes instead of 10 is not completing the activity in a reasonable time.

### 7. Communicating verbally

Expressing and understanding spoken information.

- **Can express and understand verbal information unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to speak or hear — 2 points**
- **Needs communication support to be able to express or understand complex verbal information — 4 points**
- **Needs communication support to be able to express or understand basic verbal information — 8 points**
- **Cannot express or understand verbal information at all even with communication support — 12 points**

**Example:** Hearing aids can score 2 points. Needing someone to interpret complex information (for example a medical appointment) is often 4 points.

### 8. Reading and understanding signs, symbols and words

Basic and complex written information (signs, labels, letters). Spectacles/contact lenses alone do not count as the relevant aid here.

- **Can read and understand basic and complex written information either unaided or using spectacles or contact lenses — 0 points**
- **Needs to use an aid or appliance, other than spectacles or contact lenses, to be able to read or understand either basic or complex written information — 2 points**
- **Needs prompting to be able to read or understand complex written information — 2 points**
- **Needs prompting to be able to read or understand basic written information — 4 points**
- **Cannot read or understand signs, symbols or words at all — 8 points**

**Example:** Magnifiers or screen readers can score 2 points. Cognitive difficulties needing prompting for basic written information can score 4.

### 9. Engaging with other people face to face

Social interaction face to face.

- **Can engage with other people unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs prompting to be able to engage with other people — 2 points**
- **Needs social support to be able to engage with other people — 4 points**
- **Cannot engage with other people due to such engagement causing either overwhelming psychological distress to the claimant or the claimant to exhibit behaviour which would result in a substantial risk of harm to the claimant or another person — 8 points**

**Example:** Needing encouragement to answer the door is prompting (2). Needing an experienced person with you for interactions is social support (4).

### 10. Making budgeting decisions

Simple decisions (everyday purchases) and complex decisions (bills, budgeting over time).

- **Can manage complex budgeting decisions unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs prompting or assistance to be able to make complex budgeting decisions — 2 points**
- **Needs prompting or assistance to be able to make simple budgeting decisions — 4 points**
- **Cannot make any budgeting decisions at all — 6 points**

**Example:** Someone who can buy a sandwich but cannot manage bills or a bank account without help often scores 2 points; needing help even with simple purchases scores 4.

## Mobility activities — full descriptors and points

### 11. Planning and following journeys

Ability to plan and follow routes, including psychological barriers to leaving the house. This is not the same as walking distance.

- **Can plan and follow the route of a journey unaided — 0 points**
- **Needs prompting to be able to undertake any journey to avoid overwhelming psychological distress to the claimant — 4 points**
- **Cannot plan the route of a journey — 8 points**
- **Cannot follow the route of an unfamiliar journey without another person, assistance dog or orientation aid — 10 points**
- **Cannot undertake any journey because it would cause overwhelming psychological distress to the claimant — 10 points**
- **Cannot follow the route of a familiar journey without another person, an assistance dog or an orientation aid — 12 points**

**Example:** Severe anxiety needing encouragement to leave home can score 4 points. Being unable to follow even familiar routes alone can score 12 — enough alone for Enhanced Mobility.

### 12. Moving around

How far you can stand and then move on a typical surface (like a pavement), reliably.

- **Can stand and then move more than 200 metres, either aided or unaided — 0 points**
- **Can stand and then move more than 50 metres but no more than 200 metres, either aided or unaided — 4 points**
- **Can stand and then move unaided more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 8 points**
- **Can stand and then move using an aid or appliance more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 10 points**
- **Can stand and then move more than 1 metre but no more than 20 metres, either aided or unaided — 12 points**
- **Cannot, either aided or unaided, stand or move more than 1 metre — 12 points**

**Example:** Walking from the car to the front door as your usual limit sits in the 20–50 metre range (8 unaided, or 10 with a stick/frame). Always describe reliable, repeatable distance — not a one-off push.

## What to write on your PIP form

Match your answers to descriptors and reliability — not diagnosis labels.

**Don't write:** “I struggle with walking.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days I cannot stand and then move more than 40 metres without stopping because of pain and fatigue. If I push further I cannot repeat the walk that day. I use a stick outdoors.”

**Don't write:** “Cooking is hard.”  
**Do write:** “On most days I need supervision to prepare and cook a simple meal because I leave pans on and cannot stand at the hob safely. Without someone with me I eat ready meals.”

**Don't write:** “I have anxiety about going out.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days I need prompting to leave the house to avoid overwhelming psychological distress. I cannot follow unfamiliar journeys without another person.”

Use “on the majority of days,” name aids and help, and say what happens if you try to push through.

## Worked example — how points add up

Imagine someone scores:

- Preparing food: supervision/assistance — **4**
- Washing: shower seat (aid) — **2**
- Dressing: help with lower body — **2**
- Engaging with people: prompting — **2**
- Moving around: unaided 20–50 metres — **8**
- Planning journeys: prompting to avoid distress — **4**

**Daily Living total:** 4+2+2+2 = **10** → Standard Daily Living  
**Mobility total:** 8+4 = **12** → Enhanced Mobility  

Same person, different mix of activities, different award. That is why working through all 12 matters.

## Frequently asked questions

### Do I score points for every descriptor that applies in one activity?

No. Within each activity you take only the **highest** applicable descriptor. Across activities, you add those highest scores together.

### What if I scored 7 points for Daily Living?

You get no Daily Living award. The threshold is 8. One extra point in any daily living activity can change the outcome — which is why accurate descriptors matter.

### Are PIP points based on my diagnosis?

No. Diagnosis helps explain *why* you have limits, but points come from functional descriptors. Two people with the same diagnosis can score very differently.

### Does using an aid reduce my points?

Usually the opposite for many activities: needing an aid is itself a scoring descriptor (often 2 points, or 10 points for aided 20–50 metre walking). Describe what you can do with and without the aid on most days.

### Can I get Enhanced Mobility from one activity alone?

Yes. Planning and following journeys, or moving around, can each score up to 12 points — enough for Enhanced Mobility without points from the other mobility activity.
