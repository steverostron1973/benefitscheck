PIP — Personal Independence Payment — isn't awarded based on your diagnosis. It's based on how your condition affects your ability to carry out everyday activities, measured through a points-based scoring system. Understanding how that system works can make a real difference to your claim.

This guide explains exactly how PIP points are calculated and what you need to know.

## The two components of PIP

PIP is split into two separate parts, each scored independently:

**Daily Living** — covers activities like preparing food, washing, dressing, communicating, and managing medication. There are 10 activities in this component.

**Mobility** — covers planning and following journeys, and moving around. There are 2 activities in this component.

You can be awarded one component, both, or neither — they're assessed completely separately.

## How activities are scored

Each activity has a list of descriptors — statements describing different levels of ability. You're awarded points based on the descriptor that best describes your situation on the majority of days.

Points per descriptor range from 0 to 12 depending on the activity. The key principle is that you're scored on your ability to complete the activity:

- Safely (without risk of harm)
- To an acceptable standard
- Repeatedly (as often as needed)
- In a reasonable time (no more than twice as long as someone without your condition)

If you can only complete an activity when all four of these conditions are met, you may score higher than if you can technically complete it but struggle with one of these factors.

## What the point thresholds mean

Once all activities in each component are scored, the totals determine your award level:

**Daily Living component:**
- 8 or more points → Standard rate (£72.65/month, 2026/27)
- 12 or more points → Enhanced rate (£108.55/month, 2026/27)

**Mobility component:**
- 8 or more points → Standard rate (£28.70/month, 2026/27)
- 12 or more points → Enhanced rate (£75.75/month, 2026/27)

Scoring 7 or fewer points in either component means no award for that component — even if you scored 7 in both, you'd receive nothing unless one reaches 8.

## The "majority of days" rule

A crucial and often misunderstood rule: PIP looks at how you are on the majority of days over a 12-month period, not just on your good days or your worst days. If your condition fluctuates, the assessor should consider your average across the whole period — not a snapshot of how you were on the day of assessment.

This is why it's important to describe your condition on a typical bad day, not your best day, when completing your PIP2 form or attending your assessment.

## How to check what you might be entitled to

Because PIP scoring depends on your specific activities and how your condition affects each one, the best way to understand your potential entitlement is to work through the activities one by one.
