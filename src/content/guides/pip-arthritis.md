Arthritis is one of the most common conditions among PIP claimants — but many people with arthritis, particularly older working-age adults, don't claim because they assume their condition isn't severe enough. The reality is that arthritis significantly affects daily living for millions of people, and many are entitled to PIP without realising it.

## Rheumatoid arthritis vs osteoarthritis — does it matter for PIP?

For PIP purposes, the type of arthritis doesn't determine your award — your functional ability does. Both rheumatoid arthritis (an autoimmune condition causing inflammation) and osteoarthritis (wear and tear of joints) can qualify for PIP if they affect your ability to carry out daily activities.

Rheumatoid arthritis tends to be more fluctuating with flares and remissions; osteoarthritis tends to be more consistent but progressive. Both need to be described accurately in terms of their day-to-day impact.

## How arthritis typically affects PIP activities

**Daily Living activities often affected:**
- **Preparing food** — joint pain and reduced grip strength in hands and wrists makes chopping, opening jars, lifting pots, and using kitchen equipment difficult or painful
- **Washing and bathing** — getting in and out of a bath, reaching to wash hair or feet, and gripping taps can all be affected
- **Dressing and undressing** — buttons, zips, and laces can be extremely difficult with affected hands; bending to dress lower body affected by hip or knee arthritis
- **Managing medication** — opening medication packaging can be difficult with poor grip
- **Moving around the home** — affected knees, hips, or feet can make moving around the home slow and painful

**Mobility activities often affected:**
- **Moving around** — arthritis in hips, knees, ankles, or feet directly affects walking distance and speed
- **Planning and following journeys** — pain and uncertainty about flares can cause anxiety about travelling independently

## The majority of days rule — important for rheumatoid arthritis

For rheumatoid arthritis in particular, the fluctuating nature of the condition is important. If you experience flares that significantly limit your ability on more than half your days, this should be reflected in your assessment — even if you're managing better at the time of the assessment itself.

## What award might you expect?

Arthritis awards vary widely. Someone with mild osteoarthritis in one knee may not meet the threshold for any award; someone with severe rheumatoid arthritis affecting multiple joints, particularly hands and hips, may receive Enhanced rate on both components.

The key factors are how many activities are affected, how severely, and whether you need aids, adaptations, or another person's help.

## Claiming while working

PIP is completely unaffected by whether you work or how much you earn. Many people with arthritis continue to work, often with adjustments, and are still entitled to PIP based on how their condition affects daily life outside of work.

## What evidence helps your claim

- GP letter describing the specific joints affected, your level of pain, and how arthritis affects your daily functioning
- Letters from a rheumatologist if you're under one
- Evidence of any aids or adaptations you use (grab rails, jar openers, dressing aids, walking aids)
- Occupational therapy assessment if you've had one
- X-rays or scan results aren't required but can support the severity of your condition

## Common mistakes when claiming PIP for arthritis

**Not mentioning all affected joints** — arthritis often affects multiple joints and people sometimes focus only on the most obvious one. Describe all affected joints and how each affects specific activities.

**Underestimating the cumulative effect** — arthritis pain increases with activity. Describe not just whether you can do something but how you feel during and after — pain levels, needing to rest, reduced ability to do other things afterward.

**Assuming it needs to be severe to qualify** — moderate arthritis that consistently affects multiple daily activities can qualify for Standard rate awards.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Arthritis is generally progressive, which means your condition may worsen over time. If your condition deteriorates significantly between reviews, you can request a reassessment. Keep records of any changes in your condition, new medications, or new aids and adaptations.
