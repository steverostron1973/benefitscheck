Child Benefit is a regular payment for people responsible for bringing up a child. It's one of the most straightforward benefits in the UK system — but a surprising number of families either don't claim it or stopped claiming unnecessarily when higher earners faced a charge.

## How much is Child Benefit worth?

In 2026/27, Child Benefit pays:

- £26.05 per week for your eldest or only child (£1,354.60/year)
- £17.25 per week for each additional child (£897/year per child)

Payments are made every four weeks directly into your bank account.

## Who can claim

You can claim Child Benefit if you're responsible for a child who is:

- Under 16, or
- Under 20 and in approved education or training (such as A-levels or a traineeship)

There's no income limit for claiming — but higher earners may have to pay some or all of it back through the High Income Child Benefit Charge (see below).

## The High Income Child Benefit Charge

If you or your partner earns over £60,000 a year, you'll start paying back Child Benefit through your tax return. The charge tapers gradually between £60,000 and £80,000 — at £80,000 or above, the charge equals the full amount of Child Benefit received.

Importantly, the threshold applies to individual income, not household income. Two parents each earning £55,000 can claim Child Benefit in full — but one parent earning £65,000 would face a partial charge.

## Why you should still claim even if you'll pay it back

Even if you'll pay back all of your Child Benefit through the charge, it's still worth claiming for one important reason: National Insurance credits. Claiming Child Benefit credits the primary carer with NI contributions for each year they're not working — which counts toward their State Pension. These credits stop if you opt out of Child Benefit.

## How to claim

Claim online through GOV.UK as soon as possible after your child is born or comes to live with you. Claims can be backdated by up to three months — but not further, so don't delay.
