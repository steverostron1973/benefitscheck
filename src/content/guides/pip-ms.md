Multiple sclerosis is one of the conditions most commonly associated with PIP awards — but how MS currently affects you matters more than the type on your neurology letter. Relapsing-remitting, secondary progressive, and primary progressive MS can all qualify based on functional impact on the majority of days.

## Your diagnosis doesn't determine your award

PIP is not awarded for having MS. It is awarded when MS affects 12 daily living and mobility activities. Someone in a long remission with minimal limits may score below the threshold; someone with significant fatigue, mobility problems, and cognitive difficulties may receive Enhanced rate on both components.

## How MS typically affects PIP activities

Below are the activities most often affected, with the descriptors and point values that typically apply.

### Preparing food

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points.** Perching stool, adapted utensils, or electric tin openers.
- **Needs prompting to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points.** Cognitive fog or exhaustion means you forget steps, leave the hob on, or need reminding to eat.
- **Needs supervision or assistance to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 4 points.** Someone helps because tremor or weakness makes chopping and lifting pans unsafe.
- **Cannot prepare and cook a simple meal — 8 points.** On most days you cannot cook safely even with aids.

Someone with MS who experiences significant fatigue after minimal exertion and needs to rest after washing would likely score on multiple daily living descriptors — including preparing food if cooking later in the day is no longer possible.

### Washing and bathing

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to wash or bathe — 2 points.** Shower seat, grab rails, or long-handled sponge.
- **Needs supervision or prompting to be able to wash or bathe — 2 points.** Someone nearby for fall risk, or prompting because cognitive difficulties mean you forget washing.
- **Needs assistance to be able to wash either their hair or body below the waist — 2 points.** Weakness, spasticity, or balance problems mean you cannot manage without help.
- **Needs assistance to be able to get in or out of a bath or shower — 3 points.**
- **Cannot wash and bathe at all and needs another person to wash their entire body — 8 points.**

### Dressing and undressing

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to dress or undress — 2 points.** Button hooks, sock aids, or zip pulls because of tremor, numbness, or weakness.
- **Needs either prompting or assistance to be able to select appropriate clothing — 2 points.** Cognitive difficulties or heat sensitivity mean you need help choosing suitable clothes.
- **Needs assistance to be able to dress or undress their upper body — 2 points** or **lower body — 2 points.** Spasticity, weakness, or balance problems mean someone helps with jumpers, trousers, or shoes.
- **Cannot dress or undress at all — 8 points.** On most days you cannot dress yourself even with aids.

### Managing therapy or monitoring a health condition

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to manage medication — 1 point.** Dosette boxes, alarms, or injection aids.
- **Needs supervision, prompting or assistance to manage medication — 1 point.** Someone prompts timing, checks doses, or helps with injections because of cognitive difficulties, tremor, or fatigue.

Higher therapy descriptors (2–8 points) may apply if you need help with physiotherapy, spasticity stretches, or bladder/bowel management for several hours a week.

### Managing toilet needs or incontinence

Bladder and bowel symptoms are common in MS and frequently underreported.

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to manage toilet needs or incontinence — 2 points.** Continence pads, bottles, or grab rails.
- **Needs supervision or prompting to be able to manage toilet needs — 2 points.**
- **Needs assistance to be able to manage toilet needs — 4 points.** Someone helps you on and off the toilet or with cleaning afterwards.
- **Needs assistance to be able to manage incontinence of either bladder or bowel — 6 points.**
- **Cannot manage toilet needs or incontinence at all without another person — 8 points.**

### Engaging with other people face to face

- **Needs prompting to be able to engage with other people — 2 points.** Encouragement needed to answer the door, attend appointments, or start conversations.
- **Needs social support to be able to engage with other people — 4 points.** Face-to-face contact only manageable with a familiar person present.
- **Cannot engage with other people due to such engagement causing either overwhelming psychological distress to the claimant or a risk to the claimant or another person — 8 points.**

### Planning and following journeys

- **Needs prompting to be able to undertake any journey to avoid overwhelming psychological distress — 4 points.** Anxiety about falls, relapse symptoms, or bladder urgency means you need encouragement to go out.
- **Cannot follow the route of an unfamiliar journey without another person, assistance dog or orientation aid — 10 points.** Cognitive or vision problems mean you cannot navigate new routes alone.
- **Cannot undertake any journey because it would cause overwhelming psychological distress — 10 points.**
- **Cannot follow the route of a familiar journey without another person, an assistance dog or an orientation aid — 12 points.**

### Moving around

- **Can stand and then move more than 50 metres but no more than 200 metres, either aided or unaided — 4 points.**
- **Can stand and then move unaided more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 8 points.**
- **Can stand and then move using an aid or appliance more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 10 points.**
- **Can stand and then move more than 1 metre but no more than 20 metres, either aided or unaided — 12 points.**
- **Cannot, either aided or unaided, stand or move more than 1 metre — 12 points.**

Describe how far you can walk *reliably* — safely, repeatedly, without a long recovery — including MS fatigue, foot drop, spasticity, and balance. Someone who can walk 80 metres once but then cannot walk again that day is not completing the activity repeatedly.

## The majority of days rule — relapses, remissions, and fluctuation

PIP is assessed on how you are on the **majority of days** over a 12-month period. For relapsing-remitting MS this is critical — do not describe only remission. If relapses, lingering post-relapse symptoms, or heat/overexertion worsenings limit you on more than half your days across the year, that pattern should drive which descriptors apply. Progressive MS often has a clearer baseline, but fatigue and end-of-day crashes still matter for reliability.

### How to document fluctuation

Keep a symptom diary for at least 2–4 weeks (longer if you can cover a relapse cycle), and bring a summary to your assessment. For each day, note:

- Fatigue level and whether earlier activity caused a later crash
- Walking distance before you had to stop, and recovery time
- Weakness, spasticity, tremor, numbness, or balance problems
- Bladder/bowel urgency or accidents
- Cognitive fog — memory, concentration, following instructions
- Which activities you could and could not complete
- Aids used, help from another person, or activities abandoned
- Any relapse symptoms or steroid treatment periods

Avoid “it varies” or “I’m fine between relapses.” Say: “Over the last 12 months I had two relapses lasting about six weeks each. Outside relapses, on roughly 5 out of 7 days MS fatigue and leg weakness mean I cannot walk more than 50 metres reliably and I need a shower seat to wash. During relapses I need help dressing and cannot cook at all.” That maps onto descriptors and the majority of days rule.

## What to write on your PIP form

Assessors look for frequency, reliability, and functional limits — not just your MS type or MRI results.

**Don't write:** “I struggle with walking.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days I am unable to walk more than 20 metres without stopping due to pain and fatigue caused by my MS. If I push further I need to rest for the rest of the day. I use a stick outdoors.”

**Don't write:** “Cooking is difficult when I’m tired.”  
**Do write:** “On most days I cannot prepare and cook a simple meal safely because of hand weakness, tremor, and fatigue. I need someone to cook for me or I eat ready meals.”

**Don't write:** “I get MS fatigue.”  
**Do write:** “MS fatigue on most days means that after washing and dressing I cannot prepare food or leave the house without several hours’ rest. It is overwhelming and does not improve with a short break.”

**Don't write:** “I sometimes have bladder problems.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days I have urinary urgency and frequency. I use continence pads and plan journeys around toilet access. Anxiety about accidents means I often cannot go to unfamiliar places alone.”

Use “on the majority of days” or a clear yearly pattern including relapses; cover safely, to an acceptable standard, repeatedly, and in a reasonable time; describe what happens *after* activity; name aids, DMTs, and help from others.

## Fatigue and MS

MS fatigue is different from ordinary tiredness — overwhelming, unpredictable, and often worse after heat or minimal exertion. Describe how quickly it comes on, whether you can repeat tasks, and how long recovery takes.

## What award might you expect?

Someone in remission with minimal impact may score below 8 points. Many people with significant mobility, fatigue, and daily living limits score Standard or Enhanced (8+ / 12+). Progressive MS often attracts higher awards, but RRMS can still score highly when residual symptoms and relapse time add up across the year.

## Claiming while working

PIP has no earnings or hours limit. Many people with MS work and may still qualify. Describe adaptations, crashing after shifts, and how that leaves daily activities unmanageable at home.

## What evidence helps your claim

Focus on functional impact: neurologist and MS nurse letters; GP support; DMT and medication evidence; OT assessments; aids; bladder/bowel records if relevant; a diary covering relapses and ordinary weeks. Ask clinicians to comment on walking distance, fatigue, and need for help — not only diagnosis.

## Common mistakes

**Describing only remission.** Include relapse frequency, duration, and lingering effects.  
**Not mentioning fatigue.** It often drives multiple daily living scores.  
**Not mentioning bladder and bowel symptoms.** Highly relevant to toilet needs and journeys.  
**Overstating a one-off walking distance.** Score reliable, repeatable distance.  
**Minimising help you receive.** Prompting and assistance support descriptors.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Keep diaries and clinic letters between reviews. If symptoms worsen, report a change of circumstances and request an earlier reassessment.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I get PIP for MS if I’m currently in remission?

Yes, if residual symptoms still limit you on the majority of days. Describe your baseline between relapses, and include relapse periods in the yearly picture.

### Does the type of MS (RRMS, SPMS, PPMS) decide my award?

No. Scoring is based on functional impact. Progressive MS often correlates with higher mobility scores, but relapsing-remitting MS can still attract Standard or Enhanced awards.

### Will using a stick, frame, or wheelchair reduce my walking points?

An aid can change which descriptor applies (unaided 20–50 metres is 8 points; aided 20–50 metres is 10). Aids do not disqualify you — describe what you can do with and without them on most days.

### How many points do I need, and what are the rates?

8 points for Standard and 12 for Enhanced on each component. For 2026/27: Standard Daily Living £72.65 a week, Enhanced £108.55; Standard Mobility £28.70, Enhanced £75.75.

### Should I mention cognitive problems and mood symptoms as well as physical ones?

Yes. Cognitive fog, anxiety, and depression related to MS can score on preparing food, medication, engaging with people, and journeys. Describe the combined impact.
