The High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC) is one of the most confusing parts of the UK tax system. It catches many families off guard — particularly those who assumed the 2024 threshold changes meant they were no longer affected.

## How the charge works

If either you or your partner has an adjusted net income over £60,000 in a tax year, you'll be liable for the High Income Child Benefit Charge. The charge is calculated as:

- 1% of your Child Benefit for every £200 of income over £60,000
- At £80,000 or above, the charge equals 100% of Child Benefit received

So at £70,000 income, the charge would be 50% of your Child Benefit. At £80,000, you'd pay back everything.

## It's based on individual income, not household

This is the most important and most misunderstood aspect: the charge applies to whichever individual in the household earns over the threshold — not to combined household income.

Two parents each earning £59,000 pay no charge at all. One parent earning £75,000 while the other earns nothing would pay a significant charge. This can feel unfair to single-income households, and it's been a point of political debate for years.

## The 2024 threshold change

Before April 2024, the charge kicked in at £50,000 and reached 100% at £60,000. The threshold was raised significantly — to £60,000 tapering to £80,000 — meaning many families who opted out of Child Benefit under the old rules may now be entitled to claim again.

If you stopped claiming when the threshold was lower, it's worth reviewing whether you should restart your claim.

## Should you still claim even if you'll pay it back?

Yes — in most cases. The main reason is National Insurance credits. If the primary carer in your household isn't working, claiming Child Benefit credits them with NI contributions for each year — which count toward their State Pension. These credits stop if you opt out.

You can claim Child Benefit and opt out of receiving the payments — this preserves the NI credits without triggering the charge. This is the recommended approach for households clearly above the £80,000 threshold.

## How to pay the charge

The charge is paid through Self Assessment. If you're affected, you'll need to register for a Self Assessment tax return if you don't already complete one.
