Universal Credit isn't a one-size-fits-all payment. If you have a health condition or disability that limits your ability to work, you may be entitled to an additional amount on top of your standard UC award — known as the health element.

## What is the health element?

The health element is an extra monthly payment added to your Universal Credit if you're assessed as having limited capability for work. It comes in two levels depending on how significantly your condition affects you.

**Limited Capability for Work (LCW)** — for people whose condition means they can't currently work but may be able to in the future with support. This group receives some additional support but are expected to engage in work preparation activities.

**Limited Capability for Work and Work-Related Activity (LCWRA)** — for people with the most serious conditions, where work is not a reasonable expectation. This group receives a higher additional amount (£416.19/month in 2026/27) and are not required to look for work or attend work-focused interviews.

## How do you get assessed?

To be placed in either group, you'll need to go through a Work Capability Assessment (WCA) — usually a questionnaire and sometimes a face-to-face or telephone assessment. The assessment looks at how your condition affects your ability to carry out work-related activities.

You can be referred for a WCA when you make your UC claim and report a health condition. In the meantime, you'll receive a limited capability for work waiting period before the additional amount is paid.

## Important changes for new claimants in 2026

From April 2026, the government introduced changes to the health element for new claimants. If you're making a new UC claim on health grounds from this point, the additional amount in the LCW group has been reduced. Existing claimants are protected from this change.

This makes it even more important to also check your PIP entitlement — PIP is separate from UC, unaffected by these changes, and can be claimed at the same time.

## Can you get both UC health element and PIP?

Yes — they're completely separate benefits assessed on different criteria. UC health element is about your ability to work; PIP is about how your condition affects daily living and mobility. Many people receive both simultaneously.
