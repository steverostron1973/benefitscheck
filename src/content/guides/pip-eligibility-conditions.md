A common misconception about PIP is that there's a list of conditions that automatically qualify. There isn't. PIP is assessed based on how your condition affects your ability to carry out everyday activities — not on your diagnosis alone.

That said, certain conditions are more commonly associated with successful PIP claims, and understanding how the assessment works helps you present your case effectively.

## How PIP eligibility actually works

PIP assessors score you against 12 activities across two components — Daily Living and Mobility. For each activity, you're awarded points based on the descriptor that best matches your ability on the majority of days.

The condition causing the difficulty doesn't matter as much as the difficulty itself. Two people with the same diagnosis can receive very different awards because the condition affects them differently.

## Conditions that commonly qualify

While no condition automatically qualifies, the following are among those most commonly associated with PIP awards:

**Physical conditions:** arthritis, chronic pain conditions, multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease, epilepsy, visual impairments, hearing loss, heart conditions, respiratory conditions including severe asthma and COPD, cancer, and mobility impairments.

**Mental health conditions:** severe depression, anxiety disorders, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, PTSD, and OCD — where these conditions significantly affect daily living or the ability to plan and follow journeys.

**Neurodivergent conditions:** autism spectrum conditions and ADHD are increasingly recognised in PIP assessments, particularly where they affect social interaction, communication, or the ability to manage daily tasks independently.

**Fluctuating conditions:** conditions like fibromyalgia, lupus, and ME/CFS are assessed based on how you are on the majority of days — not just on good days or bad days. Many people with fluctuating conditions underestimate their entitlement because they focus on their best days.

## What assessors look for

Regardless of your condition, assessors focus on whether you can complete activities:

- Safely (without risk of harm to yourself or others)
- To an acceptable standard
- Repeatedly (as many times as needed throughout the day)
- In a reasonable time (no more than twice as long as someone without your condition)

If you need aids, appliances, or another person's help to complete an activity, this is factored into your score — often resulting in higher points than if you can complete it unaided.

## The importance of describing your worst days

Many people lose PIP awards or receive lower awards than they're entitled to because they describe their condition on a good day. The assessment should reflect how you are on the majority of days — including bad days — over the past 12 months.
