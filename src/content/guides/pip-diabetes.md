Diabetes itself does not automatically qualify for PIP — but management demands and complications can significantly affect daily living and mobility. Awards are more common than many people realise, especially with insulin, unpredictable hypoglycaemia, neuropathy, retinopathy, or foot problems.

## When diabetes qualifies for PIP

PIP is not awarded for a diagnosis. For diabetes, the factors that usually drive scoring are:

- **Hypoglycaemia** — especially severe or unpredictable hypos that affect safety, daily activities, or travel
- **Complications** — neuropathy, retinopathy, nephropathy, cardiovascular problems, and diabetic foot disease
- **Medication and monitoring** — insulin regimes, pumps, continuous glucose monitors (CGMs), and the consequences of getting doses wrong
- **Fatigue** — diabetes-related exhaustion that limits how often you can complete activities

Well-controlled diabetes with no significant complications and minimal functional impact is unlikely to reach 8 points. Complex management, frequent hypos, or complications that limit walking, vision, or self-care often do.

## Type 1 vs Type 2

For PIP, type matters less than impact. Type 1 typically involves more complex daily management and is more likely to score on managing therapy or monitoring a health condition. Type 2 managed by diet alone rarely qualifies; Type 2 with insulin or significant complications may qualify on the same basis as Type 1.

## How diabetes typically affects PIP activities

Below are the activities most often affected, with the descriptors and point values that typically apply.

### Managing therapy or monitoring a health condition

This is often the starting point for diabetes claims.

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to manage medication — 1 point**, or **needs supervision, prompting or assistance to manage medication or monitor a health condition — 1 point.** Examples: you need a glucometer, CGM, dosette box, or alarms; or someone prompts you to check levels, calculate doses, inject, or respond to hypo/hyper alerts because of hypo unawareness, cognitive fog after hypos, or vision problems.
- **Needs supervision, prompting or assistance to be able to manage therapy that takes no more than 3.5 hours a week — 2 points** (rising to **4 / 6 / 8 points** for more than 3.5, 7, or 14 hours a week). Relevant where another person regularly helps with monitoring, injections, pump management, or treating hypos, and you can evidence the weekly hours of help.

Someone who checks glucose multiple times a day, adjusts insulin, and needs a partner to treat severe hypos several times a week is describing therapy and monitoring needs — not “just having diabetes.”

### Preparing food

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points.** Neuropathy in the hands, or needing to sit because of foot pain/fatigue.
- **Needs prompting to be able to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 2 points.** Reminding to eat on time to avoid hypos, or brain fog after hypos.
- **Needs supervision or assistance to either prepare or cook a simple meal — 4 points.** Hypo risk, vision loss, or neuropathy makes cooking unsafe without help.
- **Cannot prepare and cook a simple meal — 8 points.** On most days you cannot cook safely even with aids.

### Taking nutrition

- **Needs prompting to be able to take nutrition — 2 points.** Reminding to eat or drink to manage blood glucose.
- **Needs supervision or assistance to be able to take nutrition — 4 points.** Someone supervises meals or helps you eat during/after hypos.

### Washing and bathing / Dressing and undressing

Neuropathy, hypo-related dizziness, fatigue, and foot ulcers can affect personal care.

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance to be able to wash or bathe — 2 points** or **dress or undress — 2 points.** Shower seat, grab rails, or dressing aids.
- **Needs supervision or prompting — 2 points.** Someone nearby for hypo risk, or prompting because fatigue means you forget personal care.
- **Needs assistance to wash hair or body below the waist — 2 points**, **get in or out of a bath or shower — 3 points**, or **dress upper/lower body — 2 points each.**
- **Cannot wash and bathe at all — 8 points** or **cannot dress or undress at all — 8 points.**

### Reading and understanding signs, symbols and words

- **Needs to use an aid or appliance, other than spectacles or contact lenses, to be able to read or understand either basic or complex written information — 2 points.** Magnifiers or screen readers because of retinopathy.
- **Needs prompting to be able to read or understand complex written information — 2 points.**
- **Cannot read or understand signs, symbols or words at all — 8 points.**

### Engaging with other people face to face

- **Needs prompting to be able to engage with other people — 2 points.** Anxiety about hypos in public, or diabetes-related low mood and fatigue.
- **Needs social support to be able to engage with other people — 4 points.** Face-to-face contact only manageable with someone who recognises your hypo signs.
- **Cannot engage with other people due to such engagement causing either overwhelming psychological distress to the claimant or a risk to the claimant or another person — 8 points.**

### Planning and following journeys

- **Needs prompting to be able to undertake any journey to avoid overwhelming psychological distress — 4 points.** Fear of hypos away from home means you need encouragement to go out.
- **Cannot follow the route of an unfamiliar journey without another person, assistance dog or orientation aid — 10 points.** Retinopathy or post-hypo cognitive effects mean you cannot navigate new routes alone.
- **Cannot undertake any journey because it would cause overwhelming psychological distress — 10 points.**
- **Cannot follow the route of a familiar journey without another person, an assistance dog or an orientation aid — 12 points.**

### Moving around

- **Can stand and then move more than 50 metres but no more than 200 metres, either aided or unaided — 4 points.**
- **Can stand and then move unaided more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 8 points.**
- **Can stand and then move using an aid or appliance more than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres — 10 points.**
- **Can stand and then move more than 1 metre but no more than 20 metres, either aided or unaided — 12 points.**

Describe reliable walking distance on most days — including neuropathy pain, numbness, balance, and foot complications — not the furthest you walked once.

## The majority of days rule — hypos, fluctuations, and “good control”

PIP is assessed on how you are on the **majority of days** over a 12-month period — including ordinary management days and days affected by hypos, hypers, infections, and complication flares. A “good” HbA1c does not mean no functional limits. If hypos, foot pain, or vision problems significantly limit you on more than half your days, that pattern should drive descriptors.

### How to document fluctuation

Keep a diabetes diary for at least 2–4 weeks (longer if possible), and bring a summary to your assessment. For each day, note:

- Blood glucose patterns, hypo/hyper episodes, and whether you had warning symptoms
- How long recovery from each hypo took and what help you needed
- Insulin/medication taken and any dose-calculation difficulties
- Neuropathy pain, foot problems, and walking distance before you had to stop
- Vision difficulties affecting reading, cooking, or outdoor travel
- Fatigue and which daily activities you could or could not complete
- Aids used, help from another person, or activities abandoned

Avoid “my diabetes is fine mostly.” Say: “On roughly 5 out of 7 days neuropathy pain means I cannot walk more than 50 metres without stopping. I have hypo episodes needing help about three times a week, and on those days I cannot cook or leave the house alone until I have recovered. I need prompting to check my levels and take insulin on time.” That maps onto descriptors and the majority of days rule.

## What to write on your PIP form

Assessors look for frequency, reliability, and functional limits — not only that you “have diabetes” or a good HbA1c.

**Don't write:** “I struggle with my diabetes.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days I monitor blood glucose and adjust insulin several times a day. I use a CGM and need prompting from my partner because hypo unawareness means I miss warning signs. After a severe hypo I cannot safely cook or go out for several hours.”

**Don't write:** “I struggle with walking.”  
**Do write:** “On the majority of days diabetic neuropathy pain and numbness mean I cannot walk more than 20–50 metres without stopping. If I push further I am in severe pain for the rest of the day. I use a stick outdoors.”

**Don't write:** “I sometimes have hypos.”  
**Do write:** “I have hypoglycaemic episodes needing assistance on average three times a week. During and after a hypo I cannot prepare food safely, follow a journey alone, or manage medication without help. Recovery usually takes one to two hours.”

**Don't write:** “My eyesight is a bit worse.”  
**Do write:** “Diabetic retinopathy means that on most days I cannot read standard print without magnification and I cannot safely follow unfamiliar routes alone. This also affects seeing syringe markings and food labels without help.”

Use “on the majority of days” or clear weekly frequencies; cover safely, to an acceptable standard, repeatedly, and in a reasonable time; name devices (CGM, pump, stick) and help from others.

## What award might you expect?

Diet-controlled Type 2 with no complications often scores below 8 points. Insulin-treated diabetes with complex monitoring may score on managing therapy (often 1 point, sometimes more with substantial help). Bigger awards usually come from complications: neuropathy and foot disease on moving around; retinopathy on reading and journeys; frequent severe hypos on preparing food, journeys, and engaging with people. Combined, these can reach Standard or Enhanced (8+ / 12+).

## Claiming while working

PIP has no earnings or hours limit. Many people with diabetes work full time and may still qualify. Describe workplace hypo management and how crashes after shifts affect home activities.

## What evidence helps your claim

Focus on functional impact: GP and diabetes specialist/DSN letters; hypo records (ambulance, A&E, severe episode notes); retinopathy screening; podiatry and neuropathy assessments; pump/CGM letters; a diabetes diary. Ask clinicians to comment on hypo frequency, walking limits, vision, and need for prompting — not only diagnosis and HbA1c.

## Common mistakes

**Assuming “controlled” diabetes cannot qualify.** Complex daily management and intermittent hypos can still score.  
**Not describing hypo impact in full.** Frequency, severity, recovery time, and safety consequences matter.  
**Not mentioning complications.** Neuropathy, retinopathy, and foot disease often drive the highest points.  
**Only quoting HbA1c.** Assessors need functional limits, not lab averages alone.  
**Minimising help from others.** Prompting for checks/injections and help during hypos supports descriptors.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Diabetes can progress. New or worsening complications, more hypos, or changes in treatment may affect your award. Keep diaries and clinic letters, and report a change of circumstances if functioning worsens.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I get PIP for Type 2 diabetes?

Yes, if management demands or complications significantly limit you on the majority of days. Diet-only Type 2 rarely qualifies; insulin, frequent hypos, neuropathy, retinopathy, or foot disease are more likely to score.

### Does using a pump or CGM help or hurt my claim?

Devices are aids and can support monitoring descriptors. They do not prove you have no disability — explain what you still cannot do safely without prompting or help, especially during hypos or with complications.

### Will hypo unawareness strengthen my claim?

Often yes, if it means you need supervision, prompting, or another person to detect and treat hypos, and if hypos regularly stop you completing activities safely. Document frequency and what help you need.

### How many points do I need, and what are the rates?

8 points for Standard and 12 for Enhanced on each component. For 2026/27: Standard Daily Living £72.65 a week, Enhanced £108.55; Standard Mobility £28.70, Enhanced £75.75.

### Can diabetes and another condition be combined?

Yes. Describe the combined impact on each activity. PIP cares about what you cannot do safely, repeatedly, and in a reasonable time on the majority of days — not which diagnosis causes each symptom.
