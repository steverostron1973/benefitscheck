Diabetes itself doesn't automatically qualify for PIP — but the complications and management demands of diabetes can significantly affect daily living for many people, and PIP awards for diabetes are more common than many people realise.

## When diabetes qualifies for PIP

PIP is not awarded for having a diagnosis. It's awarded when a condition affects your ability to carry out daily activities. For diabetes, the relevant factors are:

- **Hypoglycaemia (low blood sugar)** — particularly severe or unpredictable hypos that affect the ability to manage daily activities or travel safely
- **Complications** — neuropathy (nerve damage), retinopathy (eye damage), nephropathy (kidney damage), cardiovascular complications, and foot problems can all significantly affect daily living and mobility
- **Medication management** — complex insulin regimes, monitoring requirements, and the consequences of getting this wrong
- **Fatigue** — diabetes-related fatigue is significant for many people and affects multiple daily activities

## How diabetes typically affects PIP activities

**Daily Living activities often affected:**

- **Managing medication** — insulin-dependent diabetes involves complex daily management including blood glucose monitoring, calculating doses, administering injections or managing a pump, and responding to readings. The complexity and consequences of errors are relevant here.
- **Preparing food** — managing carbohydrate intake, timing meals, and the consequences of dietary errors can affect how you approach food preparation
- **Engaging with other people** — severe or unpredictable hypoglycaemia can affect social activities and the ability to be in situations where hypos might not be manageable

**Mobility activities often affected:**

- **Moving around** — diabetic neuropathy causing pain and numbness in feet and legs, and diabetic foot complications, can directly affect walking
- **Planning and following journeys** — unpredictable hypoglycaemia can make travelling independently unsafe in some cases

## Type 1 vs Type 2 diabetes

For PIP purposes, the type of diabetes matters less than the impact. However, Type 1 diabetes typically involves more complex management demands (insulin, monitoring, hypo risk) and may be more likely to score on the medication management activity. Type 2 diabetes managed by diet alone is less likely to qualify, but Type 2 managed with insulin or with significant complications may qualify.

## What award might you expect?

Well-controlled diabetes with no significant complications is unlikely to qualify for PIP. Diabetes with frequent severe hypoglycaemia, significant neuropathy, vision loss from retinopathy, or major mobility-affecting complications may qualify for Standard or Enhanced awards on one or both components.

## Claiming while working

PIP is unaffected by employment. Many people with diabetes work full time and may still be entitled to PIP where diabetes significantly affects daily living or mobility.

## What evidence helps your claim

- GP letter describing your diabetes management, any complications, and functional impact
- Diabetologist or endocrinologist letter if you're under one
- Evidence of severe hypo episodes (hospital admissions, ambulance callouts, GP records of severe episodes)
- Diabetic retinopathy screening records if relevant
- Podiatry records if you have diabetic foot complications
- Neuropathy assessment records if relevant

## Common mistakes when claiming PIP for diabetes

**Assuming controlled diabetes doesn't qualify** — even well-controlled diabetes involves complex daily management that can qualify under the medication management activity.

**Not describing hypo impact** — if you experience severe or unpredictable hypoglycaemia, describe this fully including frequency, severity, how long recovery takes, and any impact on daily activities or safety.

**Not mentioning complications** — diabetes complications are often where the most significant PIP scoring occurs. Make sure all complications are described in full.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Diabetes is a lifelong condition that can progress over time. New complications, worsening existing complications, or changes in management may affect your award. Report significant changes to DWP and request reassessment if your condition worsens significantly.
