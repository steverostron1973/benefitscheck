Fibromyalgia is one of the most commonly misunderstood conditions in the PIP system. Because it's invisible and fluctuating, many people with fibromyalgia either don't claim PIP or receive a lower award than they're entitled to. Understanding how the assessment works for this condition can make a significant difference.

## Your diagnosis doesn't determine your award

PIP is not awarded based on your diagnosis. Having fibromyalgia doesn't automatically qualify you — and not having a formal diagnosis doesn't automatically disqualify you. What matters is how your condition affects your ability to carry out 12 specific daily living and mobility activities.

Two people with the same fibromyalgia diagnosis can receive very different awards because the condition affects people differently. The assessment is about your functional ability, not your medical label.

## How fibromyalgia typically affects PIP activities

Fibromyalgia commonly affects multiple PIP activities across both components:

**Daily Living activities often affected:**
- **Preparing food** — pain in hands, arms and shoulders can make chopping, lifting pots, and standing at a hob difficult or impossible on bad days
- **Washing and bathing** — fatigue and widespread pain can make showering or bathing exhausting; some people need to sit, use grab rails, or have help
- **Dressing and undressing** — stiffness and pain in joints can make getting dressed slow, painful, and sometimes impossible without help
- **Managing medication** — brain fog (fibro fog) can affect the ability to manage complex medication regimes safely
- **Engaging with other people** — fatigue and cognitive difficulties can significantly affect social interaction and communication
- **Planning and following journeys** — anxiety and cognitive difficulties associated with fibromyalgia can affect the ability to plan unfamiliar routes

**Mobility activities often affected:**
- **Moving around** — pain and fatigue often limit walking distance significantly, particularly on bad days
- **Planning and following journeys** — anxiety about pain flares in unfamiliar places can affect the ability to travel independently

## The majority of days rule — crucial for fibromyalgia

This rule is particularly important for fibromyalgia because it's a fluctuating condition. PIP is assessed based on how you are on the majority of days over a 12-month period — not just on good days or the day of your assessment.

If you have flares that affect you significantly on more than half your days across a year, those flares should be reflected in your assessment. Don't describe yourself based on your best days — describe the realistic picture across good days and bad days combined.

## What award might you expect?

This varies significantly depending on how severely fibromyalgia affects you. People with fibromyalgia receive awards ranging from no award (if their condition is well-managed and has minimal functional impact) to Enhanced rate on both components (where severe fatigue, pain, and cognitive difficulties affect almost all daily activities and mobility).

Many people with fibromyalgia who are in significant daily pain receive Standard or Enhanced Daily Living, with Mobility awards depending on their specific walking limitations.

## Claiming while working

PIP has no earnings limit and no hours limit. You can work full time and still be entitled to PIP if fibromyalgia affects your daily living or mobility. Many people with fibromyalgia manage to work through significant difficulty — that effort doesn't disqualify you.

## What evidence helps your claim

Strong evidence for a fibromyalgia PIP claim includes:
- A letter from your GP describing how fibromyalgia affects your daily functioning (not just your diagnosis)
- Letters from a rheumatologist or pain specialist if you're under one
- Evidence of medication prescribed for pain management
- A pain diary showing the pattern and severity of your symptoms over time
- Letters from any other healthcare professionals involved in your care (physiotherapist, occupational therapist, mental health support)

The key is evidence that describes functional impact — what you can and can't do — rather than just confirming the diagnosis.

## Common mistakes when claiming PIP for fibromyalgia

**Describing only good days** — fibromyalgia fluctuates, and people often unconsciously describe their condition at its best. Focus on the realistic average across your worst and best days.

**Not mentioning fatigue** — pain is the most obvious symptom but fatigue (including post-exertional malaise) significantly affects many PIP activities and is often underreported.

**Not mentioning brain fog** — cognitive difficulties including memory problems, concentration issues, and word-finding difficulties can affect multiple daily living activities and should be described clearly.

**Assuming you need to be housebound** — many people with fibromyalgia can leave the house on good days but are severely limited on bad days. This doesn't disqualify you — describe the full picture.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

If you're awarded PIP, you'll be reviewed periodically. For fluctuating conditions like fibromyalgia, it's important to keep records of your symptoms between reviews so you can accurately describe your condition at reassessment time. If your condition worsens between reviews, you can request a reassessment before your scheduled review date.
