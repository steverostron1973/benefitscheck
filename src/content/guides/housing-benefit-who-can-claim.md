Housing Benefit is one of the older benefits in the UK system, and for most working-age people it's been replaced by the housing element of Universal Credit. But it hasn't disappeared entirely — a significant group of people can still claim it directly.

## Who can still claim Housing Benefit

You can still make a new Housing Benefit claim if you fall into one of these groups:

- You've reached State Pension age
- You live in temporary or exempt accommodation (such as a hostel, refuge, or supported housing)
- You're in supported housing provided by a housing association, registered charity, or voluntary organisation where care, support, or supervision is provided

For everyone else of working age who needs help with rent, the housing element of Universal Credit is now the route.

## How Housing Benefit is calculated

If you're eligible, Housing Benefit is calculated based on your income, savings, household size, and the Local Housing Allowance (LHA) rate for your area. The LHA is set by the government based on local private rental market rates — it doesn't necessarily cover your full rent.

The maximum Housing Benefit you can receive is capped at the LHA rate for a property of appropriate size for your household. If your actual rent is higher than the LHA, you'll need to make up the difference yourself.

## The bedroom tax

If you're a social housing tenant receiving Housing Benefit and have more bedrooms than the government considers you need, a bedroom tax (officially called the Removal of the Spare Room Subsidy) reduces your Housing Benefit by 14% for one extra bedroom, or 25% for two or more.

## How to claim

Pensioners and those in exempt accommodation can apply for Housing Benefit directly through their local council. If you're of working age and not in exempt accommodation, you'll need to claim Universal Credit instead.
