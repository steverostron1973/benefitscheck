Child Benefit can be backdated — but only by up to three months. After that, any entitlement you haven't claimed is lost permanently. This makes it one of the most time-sensitive benefit claims you can make.

## How backdating works

When you make a Child Benefit claim, HMRC will automatically backdate your payment to the earliest possible date — either the date your child was born or came to live with you, or three months before the date you claim, whichever is later.

So if your child was born six months ago and you haven't claimed yet, you'll only receive Child Benefit going back three months — you'll have missed three months of payments that you can never recover.

## Why people delay claiming

There are a few common reasons people delay:

**Not knowing they're entitled** — Child Benefit is available to anyone responsible for a child under 16 (or under 20 in approved education), regardless of income. Many people assume an income threshold means they won't qualify — but even higher earners should claim and deal with the High Income Child Benefit Charge separately.

**Thinking the High Income Charge makes it not worth it** — even if you or your partner earns over £80,000 and you'll pay back the full amount, you should still claim to protect your National Insurance credits.

**Assuming a partner has already claimed** — only one person can claim Child Benefit per child. If you've recently separated or changed who has primary responsibility for a child, make sure a claim is in place.

## How to claim

Claim online through GOV.UK. You'll need your child's birth certificate (or adoption certificate), your bank details, and your National Insurance number. Claims for a newborn can be made as soon as you have the birth certificate — don't wait.

If you're claiming for a child who's just come to live with you (for example after a separation), claim as soon as possible from that date.

## What if you've missed more than three months?

Unfortunately there's no route to recover payments beyond the three-month backdating limit, except in very limited circumstances where you can show you had good reason for not claiming and were given incorrect advice by HMRC. These cases are rare and would require a formal complaint.

The best approach is always to claim as early as possible and let HMRC sort out any adjustments rather than waiting until you're sure about eligibility.
