Being too ill to work is stressful enough without having to navigate a complicated benefits system. The good news is that there are several forms of support available — but they work differently and have different eligibility rules, so it helps to understand what's what.

This guide covers the main benefits available if you're sick and unable to work.

## Statutory Sick Pay (SSP)

If you were employed before becoming ill, your first port of call is Statutory Sick Pay. Your employer must pay SSP if you've been off sick for four or more consecutive days and you meet the earnings threshold (earning at least £123 per week on average in 2026/27).

SSP is currently £116.75 per week and can be paid for up to 28 weeks. After that — or if you're self-employed or didn't qualify for SSP — you'll need to look at other options.

## Universal Credit (health element)

Once SSP ends, or if you weren't eligible for it, Universal Credit is often the main route for working-age people who can't work due to illness. If your condition means you have limited capability for work, you may be placed in the Limited Capability for Work (LCW) or Limited Capability for Work and Work-Related Activity (LCWRA) group.

Being in the LCWRA group means you receive an extra element on top of your standard UC payment (£416.19/month in 2026/27) and aren't required to look for work or attend work-focused interviews. This is assessed through a Work Capability Assessment.

## PIP (Personal Independence Payment)

PIP is separate from UC and is about how your condition affects your daily life and mobility — not whether you can work. You can claim PIP at the same time as UC, SSP, or any other benefit.

If your illness affects your ability to carry out everyday activities (washing, dressing, preparing food, getting around), you may be entitled to PIP regardless of your employment status or income. Many people who are off sick don't realise they can claim PIP simultaneously.

## New Style Employment and Support Allowance (ESA)

If you have sufficient National Insurance contributions from previous employment, you may be entitled to New Style ESA — a contributory benefit paid for up to 52 weeks (or longer if placed in the support group). It's currently £138.20 per week (2026/27) and can be claimed at the same time as Universal Credit.

## Council Tax Reduction

If your income drops because you're unable to work, you may become eligible for Council Tax Reduction from your local council. This is worth checking as soon as your income changes — councils apply it from the date of your claim, not backdated to when you became ill.

## What to do first

If you've just become too ill to work, the recommended order is:

1. Notify your employer and start SSP if eligible
2. Claim Universal Credit as soon as possible — don't wait for SSP to end, as UC can run alongside it
3. Consider a PIP claim if your condition affects daily living or mobility
4. Check Council Tax Reduction with your local council

The earlier you claim, the earlier payments can start — most benefits aren't backdated beyond the date of claim.

## Check what you might be entitled to

Your exact entitlement depends on your National Insurance record, household income, savings, and the nature of your condition.
