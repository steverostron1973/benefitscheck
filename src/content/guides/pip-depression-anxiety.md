Depression and anxiety are among the most common conditions in PIP claims — yet they're also among the most misunderstood. Many people with mental health conditions don't claim PIP because they assume it's only for physical disabilities. In reality, mental health conditions can significantly affect daily living and mobility, and PIP assessors are required to consider psychological as well as physical impacts.

## Mental health conditions qualify for PIP

PIP covers any condition — physical or mental — that affects your ability to carry out daily activities. Depression and anxiety can affect cooking, washing, dressing, managing medication, communicating with others, and the ability to travel independently. All of these are assessed in PIP.

The test is always functional: can you carry out these activities safely, to an acceptable standard, repeatedly, and in a reasonable time? Mental health conditions can affect all four of these criteria.

## How depression and anxiety typically affect PIP activities

**Daily Living activities often affected:**
- **Preparing food** — severe depression can affect motivation and energy to cook; anxiety can make using sharp objects or cooking appliances feel unsafe
- **Washing and bathing** — one of the most commonly affected activities in depression; lack of motivation and energy can make personal care extremely difficult on bad days
- **Dressing** — similarly affected by low motivation and energy
- **Managing medication** — depression and anxiety can affect the ability to manage medication safely and consistently
- **Engaging with other people** — social anxiety and depression can severely affect the ability to communicate and interact with others
- **Making budgeting decisions** — depression and anxiety can significantly affect the ability to manage money safely

**Mobility activities often affected:**
- **Planning and following journeys** — anxiety about leaving the house, using public transport, being in unfamiliar places, or encountering other people can significantly affect the ability to travel independently
- **Moving around** — severe depression and anxiety can cause physical symptoms including fatigue and pain that affect mobility

## The majority of days rule — crucial for mental health

Mental health conditions fluctuate. You might have periods where you're managing reasonably well and periods where you can barely function. PIP should reflect your average across the whole year — including your worst periods.

Don't describe yourself based on your best days. If you have significant periods where daily tasks feel impossible, those periods matter for your assessment.

## What award might you expect?

Awards for depression and anxiety vary widely depending on severity. Mild to moderate depression that's well controlled with medication may not meet the threshold. Severe depression or anxiety that significantly affects multiple daily living activities — particularly social engagement and the ability to travel independently — can attract Standard or Enhanced awards on one or both components.

## Claiming while working

You can claim PIP while working. Many people manage to work while experiencing significant depression or anxiety — through enormous effort, with adjustments, or in jobs that accommodate their condition. This doesn't disqualify you.

## What evidence helps your claim

- GP letter describing your mental health history, current symptoms, and how they affect your daily functioning
- Letters from a psychiatrist, psychologist, or community mental health team if you're under one
- Evidence of any therapy you receive (CBT, counselling)
- Evidence of medication prescribed for your condition
- A description from a family member or carer of how your condition affects you day to day (you can include a carer's statement with your claim)

The most important evidence is anything that describes functional impact — what you can and can't do, not just what diagnosis you have.

## Common mistakes when claiming PIP for depression and anxiety

**Describing only good days** — people with mental health conditions often describe themselves at their best, particularly in formal settings like assessments. Make sure you describe how you are on bad days too.

**Not mentioning physical symptoms** — depression and anxiety have physical manifestations including fatigue, pain, sleep disturbance, and appetite changes. These affect daily activities and should be described.

**Assuming you need to be hospitalised to qualify** — you don't need to have been admitted to a psychiatric unit or be in crisis to qualify. Moderate to severe ongoing symptoms that affect daily functioning can qualify.

**Not mentioning the effort involved** — doing things through enormous difficulty or with significant distress still counts. If you can technically do something but it causes significant anxiety or takes far longer than it should, that matters.

## PIP reviews and reassessments

Mental health conditions can improve with treatment or worsen over time. If your condition improves significantly, you should report this to DWP. If it worsens, you can request a reassessment. Keep records of your symptoms and any changes in treatment between reviews.
